# Quantum Field Theory

## 1. Motivation

Quantum mechanics describes particles, special relativity describes spacetime.  
Quantum Field Theory (QFT) unifies them: particles are excitations of underlying fields.

---

## 2. Classical Fields

### 2.1 Scalar Field
Lagrangian density:
$$
\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2} (\partial_\mu \phi)(\partial^\mu \phi) - \tfrac{1}{2} m^2 \phi^2.
$$

Equation of motion (Klein–Gordon):
$$
(\Box + m^2)\phi = 0.
$$

### 2.2 Dirac Field
For spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ particles:
$$
(i \gamma^\mu \partial_\mu - m)\psi = 0.
$$

### 2.3 Electromagnetic Field
Gauge field $A_\mu$, field strength:
$$
F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu.
$$
Lagrangian:
$$
\mathcal{L} = -\tfrac{1}{4} F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}.
$$

---

## 3. Canonical Quantization

### 3.1 Commutation Relations
Promote fields to operators with canonical commutators:
$$
[\phi(\vec x), \pi(\vec y)] = i \hbar \delta^3(\vec x - \vec y).
$$

### 3.2 Creation and Annihilation Operators
Expand field in Fourier modes:
$$
\phi(\vec x,t) = \int \frac{d^3p}{(2\pi)^3} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2E_p}} \left( a_{\vec p} e^{i \vec p \cdot \vec x - i E_p t} + a_{\vec p}^\dagger e^{-i \vec p \cdot \vec x + i E_p t}\right).
$$

$a^\dagger$ creates a particle, $a$ annihilates.

### 4.1 Interaction Lagrangian

\mathcal{L}_\text{int} = - \frac{\lambda}{4!} \phi^4.

## 5. Renormalization

Quantum fields produce divergences. Renormalization absorbs them into redefined physical parameters (masses, couplings).

---

## 6. Symmetries and Conservation

- Noether’s theorem: continuous symmetries $\to$ conserved currents.  
- Global phase symmetry $\psi \to e^{i\alpha}\psi \implies$ charge conservation.  
- Gauge invariance: local symmetry $\to$ interaction with gauge field.

---

## 7. Standard Model Framework

- **Quantum Electrodynamics (QED)**: $U(1)$ gauge theory.  
- **Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD)**: $SU(3)$ gauge theory.  
- **Electroweak Theory**: unified $SU(2)\times U(1)$.  

All formulated as gauge quantum field theories.

---

## 8. Summary
Quantum field theory provides the foundation of modern particle physics, treating particles as field excitations, interactions via gauge symmetry, and predictions calculable through perturbation theory and renormalization.
