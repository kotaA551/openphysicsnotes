# Unifying the Four Maxwell Equations

---

> **Goal.** Show how the four Maxwell equations in 3-vector form,
>
> $$
> \nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{E}=\frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_{0}},\quad
> \nabla\!\times\!\boldsymbol{E}=-\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{B}}{\partial t},\quad
> \nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{B}=0,\quad
> \nabla\!\times\!\boldsymbol{B}=\mu_{0}\boldsymbol{J}+\mu_{0}\varepsilon_{0}\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{E}}{\partial t},
> $$
>
> collapse into the covariant pair
>
> $$
> \partial_{\mu}F^{\mu\nu}=\mu_{0}J^{\nu},
> \qquad
> \partial_{\mu}\tilde F^{\mu\nu}=0,
> $$
>
> and, if desired, **one** complex equation by combining $F$ and its dual.

---

## 1. Setup: 4D notation & conventions

We work in flat spacetime with metric $$\eta_{\mu\nu}=\mathrm{diag}(1,-1,-1,-1)$$ and coordinates $$x^{\mu}=(ct,x,y,z)$$.  
We keep SI units explicit, so $$c=1/\sqrt{\mu_{0}\varepsilon_{0}}$$.

- 4-gradient: $\partial^{\mu}=\left(\tfrac{1}{c}\tfrac{\partial}{\partial t},\,\nabla\right)$  
- 4-current: $J^{\mu}=(c\rho,\,\boldsymbol{J})$  
- 4-potential: $A^{\mu}=\!\left(\tfrac{\phi}{c},\,\boldsymbol{A}\right)$  

Define the **field tensor**:

$$
F_{\mu\nu}\equiv \partial_{\mu}A_{\nu}-\partial_{\nu}A_{\mu},\qquad
F^{\mu\nu}=\eta^{\mu\alpha}\eta^{\nu\beta}F_{\alpha\beta}.
$$

Dual:

$$
\tilde F^{\mu\nu}\equiv \tfrac{1}{2}\,\varepsilon^{\mu\nu\alpha\beta}F_{\alpha\beta},
\qquad \varepsilon^{0123}=+1.
$$

:::callout{title="Component dictionary"}
$$
F^{i0}=+\frac{E^{i}}{c},\quad F^{0i}=-\frac{E^{i}}{c},\quad
F^{ij}=-\varepsilon^{ijk}B_{k}.
$$

Matrix form:

$$
F^{\mu\nu}=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & -E_{x}/c & -E_{y}/c & -E_{z}/c\\
E_{x}/c & 0 & -B_{z} & B_{y}\\
E_{y}/c & B_{z} & 0 & -B_{x}\\
E_{z}/c & -B_{y} & B_{x} & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$
:::

And from potentials:

$$
\boldsymbol{E}=-\nabla\phi-\frac{\partial \boldsymbol{A}}{\partial t},\qquad
\boldsymbol{B}=\nabla\times\boldsymbol{A}.
$$

---

## 2. Homogeneous equations from geometry

Because $F=dA$, we have

$$
\partial_{\alpha}F_{\beta\gamma}+\partial_{\beta}F_{\gamma\alpha}+\partial_{\gamma}F_{\alpha\beta}=0,
$$

which is

$$
\partial_{\mu}\tilde F^{\mu\nu}=0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{B}=0,\quad
\nabla\!\times\!\boldsymbol{E}=-\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{B}}{\partial t}.
$$

---

## 3. Inhomogeneous equations

Equation:

$$
\partial_{\mu}F^{\mu\nu}=\mu_{0}J^{\nu},\quad J^{\nu}=(c\rho,\boldsymbol{J}).
$$

### (a) $\nu=0$

$$
\partial_{i}F^{i0}=\frac{1}{c}\nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{E}=\mu_{0}c\rho
\;\;\Rightarrow\;\;
\nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{E}=\frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_{0}}.
$$

### (b) $\nu=i$

$$
\partial_{0}F^{0i}+\partial_{j}F^{ji}
=-\frac{1}{c^{2}}\frac{\partial E^{i}}{\partial t}+(\nabla\times\boldsymbol{B})^{i}
=\mu_{0}J^{i}.
$$

So

$$
\nabla\times\boldsymbol{B}=\mu_{0}\boldsymbol{J}+\mu_{0}\varepsilon_{0}\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{E}}{\partial t}.
$$

---

## 4. One complex equation

Define

$$
\mathcal{F}^{\mu\nu}=F^{\mu\nu}-i\,\tilde F^{\mu\nu}.
$$

Then

$$
\partial_{\mu}\,\mathcal{F}^{\mu\nu}=\mu_{0}J^{\nu},
$$

which packages both the inhomogeneous and homogeneous equations.

---

## 5. Cheatsheet

- Vector form:

$$
\nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{E}=\frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_{0}},\quad
\nabla\!\times\!\boldsymbol{E}=-\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{B}}{\partial t},\quad
\nabla\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{B}=0,\quad
\nabla\!\times\!\boldsymbol{B}=\mu_{0}\boldsymbol{J}+\mu_{0}\varepsilon_{0}\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{E}}{\partial t}.
$$

- Covariant pair:

$$
\partial_{\mu}F^{\mu\nu}=\mu_{0}J^{\nu},\qquad
\partial_{\mu}\tilde F^{\mu\nu}=0.
$$

- One-liner:

$$
\partial_{\mu}\,(F^{\mu\nu}-i\,\tilde F^{\mu\nu})=\mu_{0}J^{\nu}.
$$
